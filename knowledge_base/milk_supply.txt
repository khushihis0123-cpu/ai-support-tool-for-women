Milk supply concerns are among the most common reasons mothers stop breastfeeding earlier than planned. However, true low milk supply (insufficient glandular tissue or hormonal causes) is relatively rare. Most perceived low supply is actually related to feeding frequency, latch, or milk transfer issues that can be resolved.

Milk supply is driven by supply and demand. The breast produces milk in response to how frequently and how thoroughly it is emptied. The more often and more completely the breast is drained — through nursing or pumping — the more milk is produced. Conversely, if milk is left in the breast for extended periods, the body receives a signal to slow production.

Signs that suggest milk supply may genuinely be low include: slow or no weight gain in the baby, fewer than 6 wet diapers per day after day 4, baby who is consistently unsatisfied after feeds and feeds very frequently without gaining weight, and breasts that never feel full. Weight gain tracking by a healthcare provider is the most objective measure of whether the baby is getting enough milk.

Signs that are often mistakenly interpreted as low supply but do not necessarily indicate a problem include: breasts that feel softer after the first few weeks (this is normal as supply regulates), not feeling a letdown, inability to pump large amounts, baby feeding frequently (normal behavior), and baby going through a growth spurt and feeding more often.

The most effective way to increase milk supply is to nurse more frequently. Feeding at least 8 to 12 times per day, including at least once at night (when prolactin levels are highest), provides the strongest stimulus for milk production. Waking a sleepy baby to feed in the early weeks is appropriate and important.

Power pumping is a technique designed to mimic cluster feeding and stimulate increased milk production. It involves pumping for 20 minutes, resting for 10 minutes, pumping for 10 minutes, resting for 10 minutes, and pumping for a final 10 minutes — all within one hour. Doing this once or twice daily for several days in a row can help boost supply.

Ensuring a deep and effective latch is critical for milk transfer and supply. A baby who is not latching deeply may be at the breast for long periods but transferring very little milk. A lactation consultant can perform a weighted feed (weighing baby before and after a nursing session) to measure how much milk the baby actually transfers.

Offering both breasts at each feed, and doing breast compressions during nursing, can increase the amount of milk the baby takes in per feed. Finishing the first breast completely before switching ensures the baby gets the higher-fat hindmilk.

Staying well hydrated is important for milk production. Breastfeeding mothers should drink to thirst and aim for pale yellow urine as a hydration guide. Dehydration can negatively affect milk volume.

Galactagogues are foods, herbs, or medications believed to support milk production. Common ones include oatmeal, fenugreek (though evidence is mixed and it can cause digestive issues), blessed thistle, and moringa. Prescription galactagogues such as domperidone may be considered in cases of medically confirmed low supply — these require a healthcare provider's prescription and assessment.

Certain factors can suppress milk supply and should be addressed. These include: hormonal contraceptives containing estrogen (progestin-only methods are preferred during breastfeeding), certain antihistamines and decongestants (especially pseudoephedrine), inadequate sleep and extreme stress, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption.

If a mother is returning to work and pumping during the day, maintaining supply requires pumping at the same frequency that the baby would be nursing. Missing pump sessions without nursing leads to decreased supply over time. A hospital-grade double electric pump is the most effective for maintaining supply when separated from the baby.

A sudden drop in milk supply after weeks of established feeding can be caused by the return of menstrual cycles (hormones temporarily affect supply around ovulation and before menstruation), illness, stress, a new pregnancy, or starting certain medications. Supply often recovers on its own with continued frequent nursing.

Secondary lactation failure — a gradual decline in supply over weeks — usually has an identifiable cause such as insufficient feeding frequency, supplementation without compensatory pumping, or unresolved latch issues. Working with a lactation consultant to identify the cause and create a plan to rebuild supply is the most effective approach.

Supplementation with formula, if medically necessary for the baby's health and growth, does not have to mean the end of breastfeeding. A lactation consultant can help create a supplementation plan that protects milk supply through concurrent pumping, and supports a gradual transition back to exclusive breastfeeding if that is the mother's goal.
