A good latch is the foundation of successful breastfeeding. Without a proper latch, feeding can be painful for the mother and ineffective for the baby. Most nipple pain, low milk transfer, and supply problems can be traced back to latch issues.

Signs of a good latch include: the baby's mouth is open wide covering a large portion of the areola (not just the nipple), the baby's lips are flanged outward like fish lips, the chin is pressed into the breast, the nose is close to or lightly touching the breast, and the mother feels strong tugging or pulling but not sharp pain after the first 30 seconds.

Signs of a poor latch include: a clicking or smacking sound during feeding, the baby's cheeks sucking inward, the nipple looks flattened, creased, or shaped like a lipstick after the feed, the baby slips off the breast frequently, or the mother experiences pain that does not ease after the first 30 seconds.

To achieve a deep latch, hold the baby so their body is fully turned toward you, belly to belly, with the ear, shoulder, and hip in a straight line. The baby should not have to turn their head to reach the breast. Support the breast with the opposite hand in a C or U hold, keeping fingers away from the areola.

Wait for the baby to open their mouth very wide, as if yawning, before latching. Tickle the baby's upper lip with the nipple to encourage a wide open mouth. When the mouth is wide open, bring the baby quickly to the breast — not the breast to the baby. Aim the nipple toward the roof of the baby's mouth so more of the lower areola is taken in.

If the latch is painful, break the suction gently by inserting a clean finger into the corner of the baby's mouth between the gums, then try again. Never pull the baby off without breaking suction first as this can cause nipple damage.

Different breastfeeding positions can help achieve a better latch. The cradle hold is the most common but is not always the easiest for newborns. The cross-cradle hold gives more control over the baby's head. The football hold keeps the baby's body tucked under the arm and is especially helpful for mothers who had a C-section, have large breasts, or have flat or inverted nipples. The side-lying position can be useful for nighttime feeds and for mothers recovering from birth.

Flat or inverted nipples can make latching more challenging but do not prevent breastfeeding. Techniques that help include using a nipple shield (under guidance of a lactation consultant), rolling the nipple between the fingers before feeding to make it more protractile, or using a breast pump briefly before a feed to draw the nipple out.

Tongue tie (ankyloglossia) is a condition where the tissue under the baby's tongue (frenulum) is too short or tight, restricting tongue movement and making it difficult to achieve a deep latch. Signs include: poor latch despite positioning correction, persistent nipple pain, clicking during feeding, poor weight gain, and a baby who fatigues quickly during feeds. A pediatrician, ENT, or lactation consultant can assess for tongue tie and discuss whether a frenotomy (release) is appropriate.

Nipple shields are thin silicone covers placed over the nipple during feeding. They can help with flat or inverted nipples, and babies who have difficulty latching. They should be used under the guidance of a lactation consultant as they can reduce milk transfer if used incorrectly or long-term without weaning off them.

Breast compression during feeding can help a baby who latches but feeds weakly or falls asleep quickly at the breast. While the baby is actively sucking, gently compress and hold the breast to increase milk flow. Release when the baby pauses, and compress again when they start sucking. This keeps the baby engaged and increases the amount of milk they receive per feed.

If latching is consistently difficult, expressing a small amount of milk by hand before the feed can help soften the areola and make it easier for the baby to latch. It also provides a taste of milk to encourage the baby to open their mouth wider.

Seeing a lactation consultant in person for a feeding assessment is the most effective way to troubleshoot latch problems. They can observe the entire feed, assess the baby's oral anatomy, and provide hands-on guidance that cannot be fully replicated through written advice.
