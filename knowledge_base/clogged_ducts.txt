A plugged or clogged milk duct occurs when milk flow in a section of the breast becomes blocked, causing milk to back up. It is a common breastfeeding complication that can usually be resolved at home with prompt and consistent treatment. If left untreated, a plugged duct can progress to mastitis.

Symptoms of a plugged duct include a firm, tender lump in one area of the breast, localized soreness or aching in one spot, and sometimes redness over the affected area. Unlike mastitis, plugged ducts typically do not cause fever or flu-like symptoms. The mother usually feels well overall despite the breast discomfort.

The most important treatment for a plugged duct is frequent and effective milk removal. Nurse the baby frequently, at least every 2 hours, and start feeds on the affected side to ensure it is being drained when the baby's suckling is strongest. Continue to offer the affected breast at every feed until the lump resolves.

Positioning the baby so that the baby's chin or nose points toward the area of the plug during feeding can help drain that area more effectively. For example, if the plug is in the outer lower area of the breast, the football hold or a laid-back position might help direct more effective drainage there.

Gentle massage of the plugged area before and during feeding helps loosen the blockage. Use the flat of the fingers to apply firm but comfortable circular pressure on and around the lump, working toward the nipple. Massage in the shower or bath with warm water running over the breast can be particularly effective.

Applying warmth to the affected area for several minutes before nursing or pumping helps dilate the ducts and improve milk flow. A warm wet cloth, heating pad on low, or warm shower are all effective options. Warmth should be applied directly before milk removal, not for prolonged periods between feeds.

Lecithin supplementation (typically 1200 mg taken 3 to 4 times daily) has been found helpful by many mothers for recurring plugged ducts. Lecithin is a food emulsifier that may help reduce the stickiness of breast milk, making it less likely to block ducts. Sunflower lecithin is often preferred over soy lecithin. It is generally considered safe during breastfeeding but consult a healthcare provider before starting.

Do not stop breastfeeding or pumping from the affected breast during a plugged duct. Continuing to remove milk is essential for resolution. Some mothers worry that the milk is unsafe for the baby — it is not. Milk from an affected breast is safe for the baby to drink.

If the plug does not resolve within 24 to 48 hours of consistent treatment, or if the mother develops fever, chills, body aches, or increasing redness and warmth in the breast, she should contact a healthcare provider as this may indicate mastitis developing.

Recurring plugged ducts in the same area of the breast may indicate a structural issue such as a compressed duct from a bra underwire, sleeping position pressing on the breast, or a galactocele (milk cyst). A lactation consultant or ultrasound can help identify the cause.

Common causes of plugged ducts include infrequent or missed feeds, incomplete breast drainage during feeds, tight bras or clothing putting pressure on the breast, sleeping on the stomach, sudden reduction in feeding frequency, and high milk supply. Addressing the underlying cause helps prevent recurrence.

A Dangle feed, where the mother positions herself on all fours above the baby who lies on their back below, uses gravity to help drain hard-to-reach areas of the breast. While unconventional, it can be effective for stubborn plugged ducts that are not responding to standard positioning.

Vibration over the affected area — using an electric toothbrush (turned on, with the back of the head pressed against the lump) or a handheld massager — can help break up a stubborn plug. This is a commonly recommended technique by lactation consultants.
