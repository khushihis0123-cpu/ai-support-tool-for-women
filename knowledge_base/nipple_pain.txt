Some nipple tenderness is normal in the first few days of breastfeeding as the nipples adjust to nursing. However, significant, sharp, or persistent nipple pain throughout a feed is not normal and is usually a sign that something needs to be corrected, most commonly the latch.

The most common cause of nipple pain is an incorrect latch. If the baby is only latching onto the nipple rather than taking in a large portion of the areola, the nipple is compressed against the hard palate, causing pain, nipple damage, and inefficient milk transfer. Improving the latch is the first step in addressing most cases of nipple pain.

A nipple that looks flattened, creased, or shaped like a new lipstick after a feed is a clear sign of compression and poor latch. Other signs of latch-related pain include pain that lasts the entire feed, pain that does not improve in the first few weeks, or cracked and bleeding nipples.

Nipple cracks and abrasions can occur from friction or compression during feeding. To help them heal, express a small amount of breast milk after each feed and allow it to air dry on the nipple — breast milk has antibacterial properties and promotes skin healing. Medical-grade lanolin cream or purified lanolin ointment can be applied after feeds to soothe and protect cracked nipples and does not need to be washed off before feeding.

Hydrogel pads can provide cooling relief for sore, cracked nipples between feeds. They are placed over the nipple in the bra and can be refrigerated for extra comfort. They should be changed daily and the nipple should be clean and dry before applying.

Vasospasm of the nipple causes intense burning, throbbing, or shooting pain after a feed that can last minutes to an hour. The nipple may turn white, then blue or red during an episode. It is caused by reduced blood flow to the nipple and is more common in cold environments or in women prone to Raynaud's phenomenon. Treatment includes applying warmth immediately after feeds, avoiding cold exposure, and in persistent cases, consulting a doctor about medication.

Thrush (nipple yeast infection) causes nipple pain described as burning, itching, shooting, or stabbing sensations that persist between feeds, not just during. The nipples may appear pink, shiny, or flaky. The baby may have white patches in their mouth (oral thrush). Both mother and baby must be treated simultaneously to prevent reinfection. Antifungal treatment is required — contact a healthcare provider for diagnosis and prescription.

Bacterial infection of the nipple or areola can occur, especially if there are cracks or open skin. Signs include redness, swelling, warmth, or a yellow/green discharge from the nipple. This requires medical treatment with antibiotics and should be assessed promptly.

Blebs or milk blisters are small white or yellow spots on the nipple tip caused by a small amount of skin growing over a milk duct opening. They can cause sharp pain during feeding. Treatment includes warm soaks before feeding, gently rubbing the area with a warm cloth, and continuing to breastfeed through the affected duct. A healthcare provider can open a persistent bleb safely.

To prevent nipple pain, always ensure a deep latch before allowing the baby to nurse. Break the suction gently before removing the baby from the breast. Let nipples air dry after feeds when possible. Avoid harsh soaps on the nipple area as they remove the natural protective oils from the Montgomery glands on the areola.

If nipple pain is severe enough to make breastfeeding feel unbearable, a nipple shield used temporarily can allow healing while maintaining breastfeeding. However, nipple shields should be used under the guidance of a lactation consultant and with a plan to transition off them once healing occurs.

Persistent nipple pain despite correcting the latch warrants an in-person evaluation by a lactation consultant or healthcare provider. There may be an underlying cause such as tongue tie, thrush, or vasospasm that needs specific treatment.
