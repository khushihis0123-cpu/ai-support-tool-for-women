Mastitis is an inflammation of the breast tissue that may or may not involve bacterial infection. It is a serious breastfeeding complication that requires prompt treatment. Without appropriate care, mastitis can progress to a breast abscess, which may require surgical drainage.

Mastitis typically presents with a combination of breast symptoms and systemic (whole-body) symptoms. Breast symptoms include a red, hot, swollen, wedge-shaped area of the breast, intense localized pain and tenderness, and a firm lump or area of hardness. Systemic symptoms include fever of 38.5°C (101.3°F) or higher, chills, body aches, fatigue, and flu-like symptoms. The presence of fever and flu-like symptoms in a breastfeeding mother should be treated as mastitis until proven otherwise.

Mastitis most commonly occurs in the first 2 to 3 weeks after birth but can develop at any time during breastfeeding. Risk factors include a plugged duct that was not resolved, engorgement, a cracked or damaged nipple that allows bacteria to enter, sudden reduction in feeding frequency, tight or poorly fitting bra, and maternal stress or exhaustion.

It is critical to continue breastfeeding through mastitis. Stopping breastfeeding or not emptying the breast can worsen the infection and increase the risk of abscess formation. Breast milk from a mastitis-affected breast is safe for the baby. Continuing to nurse frequently is both the treatment and the prevention of worsening.

Rest is essential in the treatment of mastitis. Mastitis is a sign that the body is under significant physical stress. Mothers should prioritize rest, stay well hydrated, and accept help with household tasks and baby care wherever possible.

Antibiotic treatment is required for mastitis that is caused by a bacterial infection. A healthcare provider should be contacted promptly when mastitis is suspected — do not wait more than 24 hours if symptoms include fever. The most commonly prescribed antibiotics are dicloxacillin, cephalexin, or flucloxacillin, typically for a 10 to 14 day course. It is important to complete the full antibiotic course even if symptoms improve quickly, to prevent recurrence and antibiotic resistance.

Pain relief with ibuprofen (if not contraindicated) is recommended as it has both analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects. Acetaminophen (paracetamol) can also be used for pain and fever. Both are compatible with breastfeeding.

Apply warmth to the affected area before and during feeds to encourage milk flow and drainage. Gentle breast massage toward the nipple during feeds helps drain the inflamed area. Ensure the baby has a deep latch to maximize milk removal. If the baby cannot nurse effectively, pump after feeds to ensure complete drainage.

If mastitis does not improve within 24 to 48 hours of antibiotics and frequent nursing, or if symptoms worsen, return to the healthcare provider. A breast abscess (a pocket of pus within the breast) may be developing. An abscess presents as a very tender, fluctuant (fluid-filled) lump that may not respond to antibiotics alone and may require drainage by needle aspiration or a small surgical incision.

Mastitis can recur, particularly if the underlying cause (poor latch, incomplete drainage, stress, plugged ducts) is not addressed. After recovering from mastitis, it is worth working with a lactation consultant to identify and correct contributing factors.

Subclinical mastitis or inflammatory mastitis can occur without the obvious flu-like symptoms. If a breastfeeding mother has persistent breast pain, a recurring plugged duct in the same location, or breast milk that tastes unusually salty to the baby, this may indicate subclinical mastitis that warrants evaluation.

Mothers who have had mastitis are at higher risk of low milk supply on the affected side if significant breast tissue was inflamed or if breastfeeding was disrupted during the illness. After recovery, working with a lactation consultant can help restore or maintain supply on that side.
